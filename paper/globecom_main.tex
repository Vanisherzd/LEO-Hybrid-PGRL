% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
This paper proposes a Physics-Guided Residual Learning (PGRL)-assisted uncertainty-aware uplink-control framework for Direct-to-Satellite (D2S) IoT using Long-Range Frequency-Hopping Spread Spectrum (LR-FHSS). An SGP4/SDP4 orbital propagator provides a deterministic Doppler baseline; a Bayesian residual corrector learns timing and frequency corrections using Gaussian negative log-likelihood (NLL) loss, yielding calibrated one-sigma uncertainty bounds. These predictions drive three physical-layer control decisions: adaptive guard-band scheduling to minimize idle time, transmission timing selection via multi-attribute utility maximization, and Doppler pre-compensation by pre-tuning the carrier frequency before transmission. Trace-driven evaluation across multiple LEO orbital configurations shows that PGRL reduces guard overhead by up to 60\% versus fixed guard-band schemes and achieves residual Doppler below 300~Hz. We further realize a Semtech LR1121/LR11xx LR-FHSS transmission path and an SDR-based IQ-level D2S-like testbed (USRP B210) for CFO estimation, EVM-proxy analysis, and waterfall visualization. As preliminary hardware evidence, three TX ON/OFF trials at 868~MHz reach an automated \emph{signal-detected} state, with TX-ON exceeding TX-OFF sparse-hop occupancy by 8.88, 11.87, and 9.82~dB; this is IQ-level RF signal detection only and does \emph{not} claim standard-compliant LR-FHSS decoding or packet-error-rate (PER) measurement. The framework establishes a traceable path from orbital prediction to standards-aligned physical-layer control without requiring continuous ground-station ephemeris updates.
\end{abstract}

% ── Introduction ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Direct-to-Satellite (D2S) IoT using Low-Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites enables connectivity for power-constrained end-devices in remote areas, but the high relative velocity between terminal and satellite---up to 8~km/s for LEO---induces Doppler shifts exceeding $\pm 30$~kHz and rapid Doppler-rate changes that stress the physical layer~\cite{hurn2023lrfhss}. Long-Range Frequency-Hopping Spread Spectrum (LR-FHSS), as standardized by Semtech in the AN1200.64 specification~\cite{semtech2023lrfhss}, achieves robust uplink by distributing transmit energy across a grid of narrow frequency bins, providing frequency diversity against Doppler uncertainty. However, LR-FHSS guard bands must accommodate worst-case timing and frequency uncertainty, imposing idle-time overhead that erodes the energy budget of battery-powered IoT nodes.

Existing approaches treat orbital prediction and physical-layer control separately: TLE-based propagators such as SGP4/SDP4~\cite{vallado2007fundamentals} provide deterministic orbital state but accumulate errors over prediction horizons beyond a few minutes, while LR-FHSS systems typically rely on wide guard bands or continuous ground-station feedback to track Doppler. We propose to close this loop with Physics-Guided Residual Learning (PGRL), which uses SGP4 as a physically-consistent anchor and learns a Bayesian residual correction on top of it, producing \emph{calibrated} uncertainty estimates alongside point predictions. These uncertainty estimates enable the IoT terminal to compute a tight, statistically-justified guard interval and to pre-compensate the carrier frequency before transmission, reducing the receiver's tracking burden and improving link budget efficiency.

This paper's primary contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We propose an SGP4/SDP4-anchored Bayesian residual learner (PGRL) that produces communication-oriented pass timing, Doppler residual, Doppler-rate, and calibrated one-sigma uncertainty estimates.
  \item We design an uncertainty-aware LR-FHSS uplink controller that converts PGRL outputs into adaptive guard time, transmission-time selection, and Doppler pre-compensation, reducing guard overhead from $>60\%$ to $<5\%$ relative to SGP4-only baselines.
  \item We provide a reproducible trace-driven evaluation pipeline with explicit validation-type labeling, together with a Semtech LR1121/LR11xx + USRP B210 testbed that yields preliminary IQ-level hardware signal detection (repeated TX ON/OFF trials), without claiming LR-FHSS decoding or PER.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{LR-FHSS for Direct-to-Satellite IoT}
LR-FHSS was introduced to LoRaWAN specifically to enable direct-to-satellite IoT connectivity, providing frequency diversity against Doppler uncertainty through a grid of narrow hopping bins~\cite{semtech2023lrfhss}. Prior work has studied LR-FHSS DtS packet delivery and outage probability~\cite{ullah2022lrfhss}, designed transceiver architectures for robust D2S synchronization under Doppler and co-channel interference~\cite{jung2023lrfhss}, and evaluated miss-detection and packet-error probability with ASIC/FPGA testbeds~\cite{maldonado2024lrfhss}. These works focus primarily on the \emph{receiver side}: synchronization, detection, and PER recovery. In contrast, this work studies how orbital-prediction uncertainty can drive \emph{terminal-side} uplink control decisions before transmission.

\subsection{ML for LEO Orbit Prediction}
Machine learning has been extensively applied to improve SGP4/SDP4 propagator accuracy. Hybrid analytical-ML approaches use SGP4 to initialize orbital state, then apply EKF or neural networks to correct residual errors during unobservable intervals~\cite{kassas2022hybrid}. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) enforce conservation of energy and angular momentum as hard constraints while learning unmodeled perturbations~\cite{pinn2024satellite}. Surveys confirm that ML-based orbit prediction targets trajectory accuracy as an end, not communication-oriented outputs~\cite{li2024mlorbit}. This work differs by using SGP4-anchored residual learning not to minimize prediction error alone, but specifically to produce \emph{communication-oriented} timing, Doppler, and uncertainty outputs that directly drive LR-FHSS uplink control.

\subsection{Gap and Positioning}
Table~\ref{tab:related} summarizes the positioning of this work relative to prior art. Unlike prior LR-FHSS DtS studies, we do not target receiver design, packet delivery analysis, or gateway-side scalability. Unlike prior ML orbit prediction work, we do not target trajectory accuracy as the primary metric. Our contribution is the \emph{cross-layer connection}: converting SGP4-anchored Bayesian PGRL uncertainty into terminal-side LR-FHSS uplink control decisions.

% ── Background and System Model ───────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Background and System Model}
\label{sec:system}

\subsection{LEO D2S Channel Characteristics}
During a typical LEO satellite pass at 600~km altitude, a ground terminal experiences a maximum one-way Doppler of approximately 30~kHz at S-band (2~GHz) and a Doppler rate of $-50$ to $-150$~Hz/s near closest approach. The Doppler shift is modeled as
\begin{equation}
f_D(t) = -\frac{v_r(t)}{c}\,f_c
\label{eq:doppler}
\end{equation}
where $v_r(t)$ is the radial velocity (positive when satellite recedes) and $c$ is the speed of light. The residual CFO after pre-compensation is
\begin{equation}
f_{\mathrm{res}}(t) = f_D(t) - \hat{f}_D(t) + f_{\mathrm{osc}},
\label{eq:residual_cfo}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{f}_D$ is the predicted Doppler and $f_{\mathrm{osc}}$ is the terminal oscillator offset. In our LR-FHSS-inspired proxy, the available bandwidth is discretized into $N_f$ hopping bins. The bin width is treated as an evaluation parameter rather than a complete standard-compliant receiver model. Successful reception requires that $|f_{\mathrm{res}}|$ after pre-compensation be small relative to the bin width.

Residual Doppler is especially consequential for LR-FHSS uplink control because LR-FHSS deliberately spreads transmit energy over a grid of narrow hopping bins. When the residual CFO $|f_{\mathrm{res}}|$ is large relative to the bin width, the per-hop energy is displaced across bin boundaries, which erodes the per-bin orthogonality and the bin-localized energy concentration that a receiver relies on to detect and recombine hops. In other words, the hopping grid is the very structure that provides frequency diversity, and uncompensated residual Doppler smears energy off the intended grid positions. Tight pre-compensation that keeps $|f_{\mathrm{res}}|$ well inside one bin therefore directly protects the hopping-grid structure, which is why a calibrated, low-residual Doppler estimate (rather than orbital trajectory accuracy alone) is the quantity that matters for terminal-side uplink control. We make no claim here about resulting packet-error rate.

\subsection{SGP4/SDP4 Orbital Propagation}
SGP4/SDP4~\cite{hoots1980models} propagates Two-Line Element (TLE) sets to produce Cartesian position and velocity at any epoch. The algorithm is deterministic and computationally lightweight but accumulates RMS timing errors of 1--4~s and Doppler errors of $>5$~kHz over prediction horizons of 30--120~min, depending on TLE age. These errors are non-Gaussian and time-correlated, which limits the effectiveness of naive uncertainty estimation on top of SGP4 output.

\subsection{PGRL Architecture}
PGRL augments SGP4 with a physics-informed Bayesian neural network that operates on normalized osculating orbital elements $(a, e, i, \Omega, \omega, M)$ extracted from the SGP4 state. The network is a SIREN (sinusoidal representation network) with Gaussian NLL loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{NLL}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\left[
  \log\sigma_n^2 + \frac{(y_n - \mu_n)^2}{\sigma_n^2}
\right]
\end{equation}
where $\mu_n$ and $\sigma_n$ are the predicted mean and standard deviation for the $n$-th training target. The physics residual loss encourages conservation of Keplerian energy $E = -\mu/(2a)$ and angular momentum $|{\bf h}| = \sqrt{\mu a(1-e^2)}$, penalizing predictions that violate these invariants.

The trained model produces $\mu \pm k\sigma$ prediction intervals that are \emph{calibrated}. Table~\ref{tab:coverage} reports empirical coverage on held-out temporal test passes; the ECE (Expected Calibration Error) is 0.28\% for timing and 0.12\% for Doppler, confirming that the Gaussian NLL formulation produces well-calibrated uncertainty without post-hoc recalibration.

% ── PGRL Orbital Predictor ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{PGRL Orbital Predictor}
\label{sec:pgrl}

The PGRL predictor receives a TLE epoch and outputs a \texttt{PGRLOutput} dataclass containing:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{pass\_start\_error\_s}: predicted timing error relative to SGP4-estimated AOS;
  \item \texttt{doppler\_error\_Hz}: predicted residual Doppler after SGP4 compensation;
  \item \texttt{doppler\_rate\_error\_Hz\_per\_s}: predicted residual Doppler rate;
  \item \texttt{timing\_uncertainty\_sigma\_s}: one-sigma timing uncertainty;
  \item \texttt{doppler\_uncertainty\_sigma\_Hz}: one-sigma Doppler uncertainty;
  \item \texttt{link\_opportunity\_score}: composite score balancing Doppler, timing, and energy cost;
  \item \texttt{recommended\_guard\_time\_s}: minimum guard interval at $3.5\sigma$.
\end{itemize}

Training minimizes a weighted objective combining Gaussian NLL and physics residual regularization. The Bayesian output head maintains calibrated uncertainty without post-hoc recalibration. The online GRPO-based refinement pipeline is treated as a thesis extension and is not required for the results reported in this paper.

% ── Uplink Control ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{LR-FHSS Uplink Control}
\label{sec:uplink}

\subsection{Uncertainty-Aware Guard-Band Policy}
The guard time $g$ must satisfy $g \geq k \cdot \sigma_t$, where $\sigma_t$ is the one-sigma timing uncertainty and $k=3.5$ provides a tail probability below $5\times10^{-4}$. The \texttt{adaptive\_guard\_time} function computes:
\begin{equation}
g^* = \max\left(g_{\min}, \; k \cdot \sigma_t^{\text{PGRL}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $g_{\min}=30$ ms is a conservative implementation lower bound used in our evaluation (not a standards-specified minimum). With PGRL's $\sigma_t^{\text{PGRL}} = 16$ ms, the recommended guard is $3.5 \times 16 = 56$ ms, compared to $3.5 \times 1500 = 5250$ ms required under SGP4 uncertainty ($\sigma_t^{\text{SGP4}} \approx 1.5$ s). This reduces guard overhead from 64\% to $<5\%$ of a 240~s orbit period (Table~\ref{tab:guard}).

\subsection{Doppler Pre-Compensation}
The \texttt{compensated\_tx\_frequency} function adjusts the nominal carrier frequency $f_c$ using the predicted Doppler shift $\hat{f}_d$ and Doppler rate $\hat{f}_d'$:
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{TX}} = f_c - \hat{f}_d - \frac{\hat{f}_d'}{2}\,T_{\text{burst}}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\text{burst}}$ is the expected transmission duration. With PGRL-compensated residual Doppler of $\approx 300$ Hz (versus $>5$ kHz without compensation), the remaining frequency error is well within the LR-FHSS bin width, reducing LR-FHSS grid displacement in the proxy evaluation. PER is not reported because standard-compliant LR-FHSS decoding is outside the scope of this work.

\subsection{TX Timing Selection}
The \texttt{select\_tx\_time} function scores candidate transmission windows using a weighted utility:
\begin{equation}
U(t) = \alpha \cdot \text{link\_score}(t) - \beta \cdot |f_d'(t)| - \gamma \cdot E_{\text{idle}}(t)
\end{equation}
where $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ are tunable weights. The optimizer selects the window with maximum $U(t)$ within a 6-hour planning horizon, balancing link quality, Doppler rate, and energy expenditure.

% ── Evaluation ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
All non-hardware experiments use Monte Carlo simulation with seed~$=42$. The guard-band energy model uses simplified RX$=$12~mA and TX$=$28~mA at 3.3~V. The LR-FHSS grid proxy simulates 64-bin frequency hopping and measures orthogonality as the inner product between bin centroids under residual Doppler displacement. EVM proxy uses QPSK constellation mapping with SNR$=40$ dB.

The PGRL evaluation uses a \emph{temporal split}: all training passes chronologically precede test passes, ensuring that test predictions are made on TLE epochs not observed during training. A satellite-held-out split is used to evaluate cross-satellite generalization. The TLE age is explicitly controlled when comparing SGP4-only and PGRL residual correction, ensuring that the improvement is not an artifact of TLE staleness. SGP4 uses identical TLE input in both baseline and PGRL-assisted modes, isolating the contribution of the residual corrector. Ground truth for timing and Doppler is obtained from high-precision ephemeris (SP3 format); when SP3 is unavailable, the most recent available TLE epoch serves as proxy truth for pass timing.

\subsection{Trace-Driven Results}
\input{tables/main_results}

PGRL reduces pass-timing RMSE by $99.6\%$ versus SGP4 (4.2~s $\to$ 16~ms) and residual Doppler by $95.5\%$ ($5.5$ kHz $\to$ 235~Hz). The guard overhead drops from $>60\%$ to $<5\%$, enabling near-zero missed-opportunity probability. The QPSK EVM proxy should be interpreted strictly as a controlled RF-quality indicator under synthetic SNR$=40$ dB. Oracle pre-compensation (perfect knowledge) achieves 0.95\% EVM. SGP4-only EVM saturates at $>200\%$ due to $>5$ kHz residual CFO. PGRL pre-compensation at $\sim$300 Hz residual Doppler does not achieve full link recovery; the QPSK EVM proxy of $\approx 67\%$ at $\sim$300 Hz residual CFO indicates that constellation rotation remains measurable, confirming that hardware-level CFO tracking or residual feedback is required for robust production LR-FHSS operation. PGRL meaningfully reduces the impairment relative to SGP4-only, but does not replace full receiver-side tracking. The LR-FHSS grid orthogonality improves from $0.587$ (uncompensated) to $0.979$ with oracle pre-compensation; PGRL-compensated achieves $0.706$ at 300~Hz residual Doppler, substantially better than the SGP4-only baseline.

\subsection{Validation Scope and Limitations}
All quantitative PGRL and uplink-control results are trace-driven or proxy-simulation. This work does \emph{not} implement a full standard-compliant LR-FHSS gateway; LR-FHSS PER is not reported. The QPSK EVM is used strictly as an RF-quality proxy demonstrating that pre-compensation improves physical-layer signal metrics. The Semtech LR-FHSS TX and SDR IQ capture pipeline is implemented and has produced preliminary IQ-level hardware signal detection (Sec.~\ref{sec:hardware}); full hardware decoding/PER is out of scope.

\emph{Hardware status.} The USRP B210 capture path and the SWDM001 LR1121 firmware bring-up are operational, and the IQ-level pipeline has reached the \emph{signal-detected} state: a capture is labeled signal-detected only when an automated detector finds sparse-hop energy significantly above the noise floor (peak-to-median, burst-energy, and LR-FHSS sparse-hop occupancy thresholds, excluding the DC/LO spike) and, where available, is corroborated by UART transmit logs and a TX ON/OFF reference. Three repeated trials reached this state (Sec.~\ref{sec:hardware}). The maximum hardware claim in this paper is \emph{hardware-signal-detected}: IQ-level RF signal presence only, \emph{not} LR-FHSS decoding or PER.

To keep claims auditable, every result in this paper carries an explicit validation label, defined as follows:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{trace-driven}: evaluated against recorded TLE/ephemeris data; no hardware involved.
  \item \textbf{simulation}: physics/communication simulation in software; no hardware involved.
  \item \textbf{proxy-simulation}: a physical proxy metric (e.g., QPSK EVM) indicating RF-chain quality; not a standards-compliant PER and not hardware.
  \item \textbf{hardware-bringup}: the capture/transmit workflow runs on real hardware (USRP B210 receive path, SWDM001 LR1121 firmware); workflow validation only, \emph{not} a validated measurement.
  \item \textbf{hardware-signal-detected}: an automated detector has found sparse-hop energy significantly above the noise floor (peak-to-median, burst-energy, and sparse-hop occupancy thresholds, excluding the DC/LO spike), corroborated where available by UART transmit logs and a TX ON/OFF reference. This is the highest label used in this paper (Sec.~\ref{sec:hardware}); it denotes IQ-level RF signal presence only and \emph{not} LR-FHSS decoding or PER.
\end{itemize}

\section{Hardware Validation}
\label{sec:hardware}

\subsection{Semtech LR1121 TX Path}
The Semtech LR1121/LR11xx dev-kit running SWDM001 firmware~\cite{swdm001} produces a standards-compliant LR-FHSS transmission. The \texttt{semtech\_validation/tx\_config\_from\_pgrl.py} script converts a \texttt{PGRLOutput} into a TX configuration JSON specifying center frequency, TX power, hopping grid parameters, guard time, and Doppler pre-compensation flags. The dry-run mode validates the config generation without hardware; live TX capture over the SDR receive path enables IQ-level signal-quality assessment.

\subsection{SDR IQ Testbed}
A USRP B210 captures IQ samples from the LR1121 transmitter through the TX/RX receive path. The present artifact reports IQ-level signal detection; calibrated conducted measurements with attenuator-controlled coupling are left for future RF characterization. The SDR captures IQ samples at 1~MHz bandwidth, which are processed by the CFO estimator (phase-derivative method), EVM proxy (QPSK constellation), and waterfall visualizer. The SDR pipeline also runs in synthetic-IQ mode for dry-run validation of all signal processing blocks.

\subsection{Preliminary IQ-Level Signal Detection}
\label{sec:hw-results}
Using the SWDM001 LR1121 transmitter (RF $=868$~MHz, TX power 10~dBm, 1~s packet interval) and a USRP B210 receiver (TX/RX antenna, 1~Msps, 45~dB gain, 10~s capture), the pipeline was run with a transmitter-on/off differential protocol. A capture is labeled \emph{signal-detected} only when the LR-FHSS-aware detector finds sparse-hop time-frequency occupancy significantly above the per-bin noise floor (DC/LO spike excluded), corroborated by UART transmit logs and a TX-OFF reference. In three TX ON/OFF trials at 868~MHz, TX-ON consistently exceeded TX-OFF sparse-hop occupancy by 8.88, 11.87, and 9.82~dB, with all three reaching \emph{signal-detected} (Fig.~\ref{fig:hw_lrfhss}). This constitutes IQ-level RF signal detection only; it is \emph{not} standard-compliant LR-FHSS decoding and \emph{not} a PER measurement.

% ── Conclusion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented PGRL-assisted uncertainty-aware uplink control for LR-FHSS-based D2S IoT. On trace-driven evaluation, the PGRL predictor achieves calibrated, sub-300~Hz residual Doppler and 16~ms timing RMSE, enabling tight guard bands, accurate pre-compensation, and energy-efficient TX timing. Trace-driven evaluation demonstrates $>95\%$ reduction in guard overhead and Doppler error relative to SGP4-only baselines. The hardware pipeline (Semtech LR1121 + USRP B210) has produced preliminary IQ-level signal detection, with three repeated TX ON/OFF trials at 868~MHz reaching the \emph{signal-detected} state (8.88, 11.87, 9.82~dB ON/OFF occupancy deltas). This validates RF signal presence only; standard-compliant LR-FHSS decoding and PER measurement remain out of scope and are left to future work.
