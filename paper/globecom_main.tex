% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Long-Range Frequency-Hopping Spread Spectrum (LR-FHSS) direct-to-satellite IoT terminals must schedule short uplink opportunities under LEO timing error and fast Doppler variation, where conservative guards waste energy and residual carrier-frequency offset (CFO) displaces hops from the intended frequency grid. This paper proposes Physics-Guided Residual Learning (PGRL)-assisted uncertainty-aware LR-FHSS uplink control. SGP4/SDP4 provides the deterministic pass and Doppler baseline, and a Bayesian residual learner predicts timing/Doppler corrections with calibrated one-sigma uncertainty. The controller converts these outputs into adaptive guard time, TX timing selection, and Doppler pre-compensation for a standards-compatible LR-FHSS TX path. In trace-driven and proxy evaluation, PGRL reduces pass-timing RMSE from 4.2~s to 16~ms, residual Doppler from 5.5~kHz to 235~Hz, and guard overhead from 2.41\% to 0.23\%. As preliminary hardware evidence, a Semtech LR1121/LR11xx transmitter and USRP B210 receiver produce three 868~MHz TX ON/OFF trials labeled \emph{signal-detected}, with sparse-hop occupancy deltas of 8.88, 11.87, and 9.82~dB. This hardware result is IQ-level RF signal detection only and does \emph{not} claim LR-FHSS decoding or packet-error-rate (PER) measurement.
\end{abstract}

% ── Introduction ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Direct-to-Satellite (D2S) IoT using Low-Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites enables connectivity for power-constrained end-devices in remote areas, but it turns orbit prediction error into a radio-control problem. During a LEO pass, high relative velocity can induce Doppler shifts of tens of kilohertz and rapid Doppler-rate changes~\cite{3gpp2019tr38821,hurn2023lrfhss}. Long-Range Frequency-Hopping Spread Spectrum (LR-FHSS), as standardized in LoRaWAN regional parameters and described in Semtech's AN1200.64 note~\cite{loraalliance2021rp002,semtech2023lrfhss}, improves uplink robustness by spreading energy across narrow hopping bins. That same grid makes terminal-side timing and frequency errors costly: seconds of pass-timing uncertainty become receive/transmit guard overhead, while kHz-level residual CFO moves hop energy away from the intended bins.

Today, the orbit-prediction and LR-FHSS control problems are usually handled on separate sides of the system. TLE-based propagators such as SGP4/SDP4~\cite{vallado2007fundamentals} provide a deterministic orbital state, but their residual timing and Doppler errors grow with prediction horizon and TLE age. LR-FHSS receiver and reliability studies, in turn, typically focus on synchronization, detection, packet delivery, or replication after a transmission is already present. The missing interface is terminal-side control: prior work improves trajectory accuracy or receiver-side robustness, whereas we need calibrated timing and Doppler uncertainty that can directly decide when to wake, when to transmit, and how much carrier pre-compensation to apply.

We address this interface with Physics-Guided Residual Learning (PGRL). PGRL keeps SGP4 as the deterministic physical anchor and learns a Bayesian residual correction that outputs communication-oriented pass timing, Doppler, Doppler-rate, and uncertainty estimates rather than a generic orbit-only endpoint. An LR-FHSS-based uplink controller then maps those estimates into guard time, TX timing, and Doppler pre-compensation decisions before transmission.

This paper's primary contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We propose an SGP4/SDP4-anchored Bayesian residual learner (PGRL) that produces communication-oriented pass timing, Doppler residual, Doppler-rate, and calibrated one-sigma uncertainty estimates.
  \item We design an uncertainty-aware LR-FHSS uplink controller that converts PGRL outputs into adaptive guard time, transmission-time selection, and Doppler pre-compensation for a standards-compatible TX path.
  \item We provide a reproducible trace-driven evaluation pipeline with explicit validation-type labeling, together with a Semtech LR1121/LR11xx + USRP B210 testbed that yields preliminary IQ-level hardware signal detection (repeated TX ON/OFF trials), without claiming LR-FHSS decoding or PER.
\end{itemize}

% ── Related Work ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{LR-FHSS for Direct-to-Satellite IoT}
LR-FHSS extends LoRaWAN with a narrowband frequency-hopping uplink whose regional parameters specify header repetition, coding-rate choices, hopping grids, and operating channel widths~\cite{loraalliance2021rp002}. Semtech's system-performance note and the LR-FHSS overview by Boquet \emph{et al.} motivate the waveform for high-capacity, long-range LPWAN and satellite-IoT scenarios~\cite{semtech2023lrfhss,boquet2021lrfhss}. Analytical D2S studies model packet delivery and outage for LR-FHSS satellite uplinks~\cite{ullah2022lrfhss}. Closest to terminal policy, Alvarez \emph{et al.} study trajectory-aware LoRa/LR-FHSS uplink policies and show that satellite trajectory information can improve scalability and energy behavior~\cite{alvarez2022uplink}. These works establish LR-FHSS as a credible D2S-IoT PHY/MAC candidate, but their trajectory inputs are not converted into calibrated timing and Doppler uncertainty for terminal-side guard and carrier decisions.

\subsection{Receiver, Transceiver, and Reliability Studies}
Recent LR-FHSS work is strongest on the receiver and reliability side. Jung \emph{et al.} design LR-FHSS transceivers for D2S-IoT and evaluate detection/PER behavior under satellite-channel impairments~\cite{jung2023lrfhss,jung2025lrfhss}. Other studies improve header recovery~\cite{knop2024header}, contention resolution~\cite{santana2024acrda}, message replication~\cite{rathi2024replication}, or probabilistic allocation of payload code rate and header copies~\cite{farhat2025probabilistic}. Energy studies further characterize LR-FHSS device current consumption~\cite{sanchez2024energy}. These are complementary to our direction: they mainly improve gateway-side detection, collision recovery, redundancy selection, or device-energy models after an LR-FHSS frame structure is chosen. This paper asks how the terminal should choose when to transmit, how much guard time to reserve, and how to pre-compensate Doppler before transmission.

\subsection{Orbit Prediction and ML Residual Correction}
The orbital baseline for this paper is the SGP4/SDP4 family introduced in SPACETRACK Report No.~3 and refined in later implementation guidance~\cite{hoots1980models,vallado2006revisiting}; Vallado's astrodynamics text remains a standard reference for the underlying propagation and coordinate machinery~\cite{vallado2007fundamentals}. Machine-learning orbit work increasingly treats SGP4 or a numerical propagator as a physics anchor and learns residual corrections or uncertainty-aware fusions. Peng and Bai use Gaussian processes and learned corrections to improve orbit prediction and fuse learned outputs with classical predictions~\cite{peng2019gp,peng2021fusion}. Differentiable SGP4 enables gradient-based correction around the analytical propagator~\cite{acciarini2025dsgp4}, and recent surveys position these methods as orbit-determination, orbit-prediction, or density-modeling tools~\cite{caldas2024mlorbit}. The common objective is trajectory accuracy; communication-control outputs such as guard time, residual CFO risk, and pass-window utility are typically not the primary endpoint.

\subsection{Doppler and Uncertainty-Aware Satellite IoT Control}
NTN standards work already recognizes that satellite ephemeris, GNSS position, timing advance, and frequency correction are central to non-terrestrial access~\cite{3gpp2019tr38821}. For LPWAN D2S links, Doppler studies show that LoRa-family waveforms face strong frequency shifts and Doppler-rate stress in LEO passes~\cite{hurn2023lrfhss}, while trajectory-aware DtS policies use distance, Doppler, or provisioned TLE information to choose transmission parameters~\cite{alvarez2022uplink}. These strands support prediction-driven uplink control, but they generally assume deterministic trajectory knowledge, network-side scheduling, or receiver-side tracking rather than a calibrated uncertainty distribution produced by an SGP4-anchored learner.

\subsection{Gap Summary}
Prior LR-FHSS research focuses on waveform operation, D2S packet delivery, receiver/transceiver design, replication, and reliability recovery. Prior ML orbit-prediction research focuses on improving ephemeris or state accuracy. This paper connects the two: it converts SGP4-anchored Bayesian residual predictions into communication-oriented timing and Doppler residuals with calibrated uncertainty, then uses those quantities for terminal-side LR-FHSS guard-time adaptation, TX timing selection, and Doppler pre-compensation.

% ── Background and System Model ───────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Background and System Model}
\label{sec:system}

This section defines the channel quantities that the predictor and controller exchange: pass timing, residual Doppler, Doppler rate, and calibrated uncertainty.

\subsection{LEO D2S Channel Characteristics}
During a typical LEO satellite pass at 600~km altitude, a ground terminal experiences a maximum one-way Doppler of approximately 30~kHz at S-band (2~GHz) and a Doppler rate of $-50$ to $-150$~Hz/s near closest approach. The Doppler shift is modeled as
\begin{equation}
f_D(t) = -\frac{v_r(t)}{c}\,f_c
\label{eq:doppler}
\end{equation}
where $v_r(t)$ is the radial velocity (positive when satellite recedes) and $c$ is the speed of light. The residual CFO after pre-compensation is
\begin{equation}
f_{\mathrm{res}}(t) = f_D(t) - \hat{f}_D(t) + f_{\mathrm{osc}},
\label{eq:residual_cfo}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{f}_D$ is the predicted Doppler and $f_{\mathrm{osc}}$ is the terminal oscillator offset. In our LR-FHSS-inspired proxy, the available bandwidth is discretized into $N_f$ hopping bins. The bin width is treated as an evaluation parameter rather than a complete standard-compliant receiver model. The proxy therefore rewards control decisions that keep $|f_{\mathrm{res}}|$ small relative to the bin width.

Residual Doppler is especially consequential for LR-FHSS uplink control because LR-FHSS deliberately spreads transmit energy over a grid of narrow hopping bins. When the residual CFO $|f_{\mathrm{res}}|$ is large relative to the bin width, the per-hop energy is displaced across bin boundaries, which erodes the per-bin orthogonality and the bin-localized energy concentration that a receiver relies on to detect and recombine hops. In other words, the hopping grid is the very structure that provides frequency diversity, and uncompensated residual Doppler smears energy off the intended grid positions. Tight pre-compensation that keeps $|f_{\mathrm{res}}|$ well inside one bin therefore directly protects the hopping-grid structure, which is why calibrated residual Doppler, rather than orbital trajectory accuracy alone, is the quantity that matters for terminal-side uplink control.

\subsection{SGP4/SDP4 Orbital Propagation}
SGP4/SDP4~\cite{hoots1980models} propagates Two-Line Element (TLE) sets to produce Cartesian position and velocity at any epoch. The algorithm is deterministic and computationally lightweight but accumulates RMS timing errors of 1--4~s and Doppler errors of $>5$~kHz over prediction horizons of 30--120~min, depending on TLE age. These errors are non-Gaussian and time-correlated, which limits the effectiveness of naive uncertainty estimation on top of SGP4 output.

\subsection{PGRL Architecture}
PGRL augments SGP4 with a physics-informed Bayesian neural network that operates on normalized osculating orbital elements $(a, e, i, \Omega, \omega, M)$ extracted from the SGP4 state. The released configuration uses `TrajectoryPINN' with a 6-hour planning horizon, 60-second query spacing, and a shared SGP4 baseline context from the same TLE set. The network is trained with Gaussian NLL loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{NLL}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\left[
  \log\sigma_n^2 + \frac{(y_n - \mu_n)^2}{\sigma_n^2}
\right]
\end{equation}
where $\mu_n$ and $\sigma_n$ are the predicted mean and standard deviation for the $n$-th training target. The physics residual regularization encourages conservation of Keplerian energy $E = -\mu/(2a)$ and angular momentum $|{\bf h}| = \sqrt{\mu a(1-e^2)}$, penalizing predictions that violate these invariants.

The trained model produces $\mu \pm k\sigma$ prediction intervals that are \emph{calibrated}. Table~\ref{tab:coverage} reports empirical coverage on held-out temporal test passes; the ECE (Expected Calibration Error) is 0.28\% for timing and 0.12\% for Doppler, confirming that the Gaussian NLL formulation produces well-calibrated uncertainty without post-hoc recalibration.

% ── Uplink Control ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{LR-FHSS Uplink Control}
\label{sec:uplink}

The uplink controller consumes \texttt{PGRLOutput} fields directly. Timing uncertainty sets the guard interval, mean Doppler sets the pre-compensated carrier frequency, Doppler-rate penalizes rapidly varying windows during timing selection, and the composite link score ranks candidate transmission windows.

\subsection{Uncertainty-Aware Guard-Band Policy}
The guard time $g$ must satisfy $g \geq k \cdot \sigma_t$, where $\sigma_t$ is the one-sigma timing uncertainty and $k=3.5$ provides a tail probability below $5\times10^{-4}$. The \texttt{adaptive\_guard\_time} function computes:
\begin{equation}
g^* = \max\left(g_{\min}, \; k \cdot \sigma_t^{\text{PGRL}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $g_{\min}=30$ ms is a conservative implementation lower bound used in our evaluation (not a standards-specified minimum). With PGRL's $\sigma_t^{\text{PGRL}} = 16$ ms, the recommended guard is $3.5 \times 16 = 56$ ms, compared to $3.5 \times 1500 = 5250$ ms required under SGP4 uncertainty ($\sigma_t^{\text{SGP4}} \approx 1.5$ s). This reduces guard overhead from 64\% to $<5\%$ of a 240~s orbit period (Table~\ref{tab:guard}).

\subsection{Doppler Pre-Compensation}
The \texttt{compensated\_tx\_frequency} function adjusts the nominal carrier frequency $f_c$ using the predicted Doppler shift $\hat{f}_d$:
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{TX}} = f_c - \alpha \hat{f}_d
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the pre-compensation ratio. Doppler-rate is still used by the controller, but through \texttt{select\_tx\_time}: high-$|f_d'|$ windows are penalized so transmissions avoid rapidly varying carrier conditions. With PGRL-compensated residual Doppler of $\approx 300$ Hz (versus $>5$ kHz without compensation), the remaining frequency error is well within the LR-FHSS bin width, reducing LR-FHSS grid displacement in the proxy evaluation.

\subsection{TX Timing Selection}
The \texttt{select\_tx\_time} function scores candidate transmission windows using a weighted utility:
\begin{equation}
U(t) = \alpha \cdot \text{link\_score}(t) - \beta \cdot |f_d'(t)| - \gamma \cdot E_{\text{idle}}(t)
\end{equation}
where $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ are tunable weights. The optimizer selects the window with maximum $U(t)$ within a 6-hour planning horizon, balancing link quality, Doppler rate, and energy expenditure.

% ── Evaluation ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

We evaluate the full chain in stages: trace-driven prediction quality, simulation/proxy control metrics, and a separate IQ-level hardware signal-detection check.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
All non-hardware experiments use Monte Carlo simulation with seed~$=42$. The guard-band energy model uses simplified RX$=$12~mA and TX$=$28~mA at 3.3~V. The LR-FHSS grid proxy simulates 64-bin frequency hopping and measures orthogonality as the inner product between bin centroids under residual Doppler displacement. EVM proxy uses QPSK constellation mapping with SNR$=40$ dB.

\emph{Dataset and provenance.} Released exp1 config: TLE input from \texttt{data/tle/}; 20 satellites (53$^\circ$/72$^\circ$/98$^\circ$); 6-hour horizon; 60-second query spacing; 100 test passes. Labels are pass timing, Doppler, and Doppler-rate against a shared SGP4 baseline. Reference timing/Doppler uses SP3 where available and latest-TLE proxy timing otherwise. Leakage control uses chronological train-before-test separation, held-out-satellite evaluation, identical TLE input for SGP4-only and PGRL-assisted runs, and explicit TLE-age control. Exact dataset expansion remains script-configurable. Hardware evidence uses the repeatability artifact with summary JSON/CSV, UART logs, and per-run ON/OFF analyses.

SGP4-only and PGRL-assisted runs share the same TLE input, query epochs, and deterministic SGP4 baseline, so labels remain residuals relative to that shared baseline rather than an independent future feed.

\subsection{Trace-Driven Results}
\input{tables/main_results}

PGRL reduces pass-timing RMSE by $99.6\%$ versus SGP4 (4.2~s $\to$ 16~ms) and residual Doppler by $95.5\%$ ($5.5$ kHz $\to$ 235~Hz). The modeled guard overhead drops from 2.41\% to 0.23\%, enabling near-zero missed-opportunity probability under the calibrated policy. The QPSK EVM proxy is a controlled RF-quality indicator under synthetic SNR$=40$ dB: Oracle pre-compensation achieves 0.95\% EVM, SGP4-only saturates at $>200\%$, and PGRL remains at $\approx 67\%$. PGRL therefore reduces impairment relative to SGP4-only, but does not replace receiver-side tracking. In the LR-FHSS grid proxy, the JSON artifact reports PGRL orthogonality of 0.954 at 300~Hz residual Doppler and 0.705 at 500~Hz; this is a grid-alignment proxy, not an LR-FHSS decoding or PER result.

\subsection{Validation Scope and Limitations}
All quantitative PGRL and uplink-control results are trace-driven or proxy-simulation. This work does \emph{not} implement a full standard-compliant LR-FHSS gateway, and LR-FHSS PER is not reported. QPSK EVM is used strictly as an RF-quality proxy. The Semtech LR-FHSS TX and SDR IQ-capture pipeline has produced preliminary IQ-level hardware signal detection (Sec.~\ref{sec:hardware}).

\emph{Hardware status.} The USRP B210 capture path and SWDM001 LR1121 firmware bring-up are operational, and the IQ-level pipeline has reached \emph{hardware-signal-detected} in three repeated TX ON/OFF trials (Sec.~\ref{sec:hardware}). We use four validation labels: \textbf{trace-driven}, \textbf{simulation}, \textbf{proxy-simulation}, and \textbf{hardware-signal-detected}. They separate orbit/control accuracy, LR-FHSS grid or QPSK-EVM indicators, and IQ-level TX ON/OFF RF evidence; none implies packet decoding, gateway interoperability, or PER.

% ── Hardware Validation ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Hardware Validation}
\label{sec:hardware}

\subsection{Semtech LR1121 TX Path}
The Semtech LR1121 dev kit running SWDM001 firmware~\cite{swdm001} provides a standards-compatible LR-FHSS TX path. A validation script converts PGRL output into a TX configuration JSON specifying center frequency, TX power, hopping-grid parameters, guard time, and Doppler pre-compensation flags.

\subsection{SDR IQ Testbed}
A USRP B210 captures IQ samples from the LR1121 transmitter through the TX/RX receive path. The present artifact reports IQ-level signal detection only. The SDR captures IQ at 1~MHz bandwidth and feeds the sparse-hop detector together with auxiliary CFO, EVM-proxy, and spectral-occupancy analysis.

\subsection{Preliminary IQ-Level Signal Detection}
\label{sec:hw-results}
Using the SWDM001 LR1121 transmitter (RF $=868$~MHz, TX power 10~dBm, 1~s packet interval) and a USRP B210 receiver (TX/RX antenna, 1~Msps, 45~dB gain, 10~s capture), the pipeline was run with a transmitter-on/off differential protocol. In three TX ON/OFF trials at 868~MHz, TX-ON exceeded TX-OFF sparse-hop occupancy by 8.88, 11.87, and 9.82~dB, with all three reaching \emph{signal-detected} (Fig.~\ref{fig:hw_lrfhss_detection}).

Detector gate: \emph{signal-detected} requires UART-confirmed TX activity, ON/OFF occupancy dominance, exclusion of DC/LO guard bins, and LR-FHSS candidate score above threshold. In the curated artifacts, this means positive UART packet count, score above 0.50, and TX-ON occupancy that exceeds the TX-OFF reference under shared detector math.

The ON/OFF reference suppresses static spurs and receiver artifacts, so the reported delta is transmitter-dependent rather than environment-only. Because this artifact does not include a standard-compliant LR-FHSS gateway decoder, it validates RF presence and detector consistency rather than packet recovery or PER.

% ── Conclusion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented PGRL-assisted uncertainty-aware uplink control for LR-FHSS-based D2S IoT. Trace-driven evaluation achieves 16~ms timing RMSE, sub-300~Hz residual Doppler, and $>95\%$ reduction in guard overhead and Doppler error relative to SGP4-only baselines. The LR1121 + USRP B210 pipeline provides preliminary IQ-level signal detection in three repeated TX ON/OFF trials at 868~MHz (8.88, 11.87, 9.82~dB deltas); standards-compliant LR-FHSS decoding and PER measurement remain future work.
